Chapter 13

Productivity Strategies

Building Systems That Engage Rather Than Demand

The Intelligence Problem in Productivity Standard productivity systems were designed for a reasonably consistent, linearly operating brain with functional executive management and a moderate tolerance for routine process. Many of them — Getting Things Done, time-blocking frameworks, habit-stacking systems, the various inbox-zero methodologies — contain genuinely useful structural insights. They also share a common failure mode for high-IQ ADHD adults: they are insufficiently interesting to maintain. This is not a trivial complaint. For a brain that requires genuine engagement to sustain involvement, a productivity system that becomes routine is a productivity system that stops being used. The elaborate setup, the initial excitement of a new approach, the first week of disciplined implementation — and then the gradual, familiar drift as the novelty depletes and the system becomes just another obligation the brain doesn't want to meet. High-IQ ADHD adults have often been through this cycle many times. They have a graveyard of productivity systems that worked briefly and then didn't. They may have concluded, from this pattern, that they are incapable of sustained organization. That conclusion is wrong. The right conclusion is that they have been applying systems designed for a different operating architecture — and that what they need are systems specifically designed for high cognitive capacity paired with variable executive engagement and an aggressive novelty requirement. This chapter is about building those systems. Principle 1: Systems Should Flex, Not Perfect The most common failure mode in ADHD productivity system design is the pursuit of the perfect system — the complete, elegant, comprehensive organizational architecture that handles every category of task and commitment and produces reliable output regardless of activation state. This system does not exist. More importantly, the pursuit of it is itself an ADHD trap: the design of a perfect system is intellectually engaging in a way that the use of any actual system is not. The high-IQ ADHD brain can spend enormous energy — happily, with full focus — designing and redesigning organizational frameworks that never get used, because designing is interesting and using is routine.

The antidote is designing explicitly for flexibility and for failure recovery rather than for perfection. A good ADHD productivity system has the following properties: It works at minimum viable engagement. When activation is low, when the day has gone sideways, when everything feels hard — the system should still be usable. This means it is simple enough that using it doesn't require significant executive function. A complex system that only works when executive function is fully available is the least useful kind. It accommodates variation without collapsing. Missed days, derailed weeks, and periods of low output are predictable features of the ADHD pattern. A system that requires consistent daily compliance to remain functional will fail regularly. A system with a simple reset protocol that restores function after disruption is resilient rather than fragile. It is designed for resumption, not maintenance. The question to ask of any system is not can I maintain this? but can I easily resume this after I inevitably don't? The simpler the resumption path, the more durable the system in practice. It is interesting enough to use. This is not a luxury criterion. For a high-IQ ADHD brain, a system that has zero inherent interest will not be used. Building in small elements of engagement — visual design, a review ritual with a good beverage, a gamification element, a tool with aesthetic satisfaction — is legitimate system design, not self-indulgence. Principle 2: Separate Idea Capture from Execution The high-IQ ADHD brain generates ideas continuously, often at volume and velocity that feels difficult to manage. Ideas arrive during tasks, during conversations, during sleep, during exercise, during unrelated work. They feel urgent — as if not capturing them immediately risks losing them forever. This urgency is partly real (ADHD working memory is unreliable) and partly the ADHD novelty system's enthusiasm for new inputs. The problem occurs when idea capture is allowed to merge with execution time. A new idea that arrives during deep work, if not managed carefully, can redirect the session entirely. The new idea is interesting. The current task may not be. The ADHD interest-based system will follow the more interesting stimulus unless there is a structural reason not to. The solution is architectural separation: a designated capture space for ideas that is distinct from the execution space for current work. The parking lot A parking lot is a specific, accessible capture location — a notebook, a running document, a dedicated notes section — for ideas that arrive at inappropriate moments. When an idea

appears during a focus block, it goes into the parking lot immediately with enough detail to be recoverable, and the current work resumes. The parking lot does several things simultaneously: it honors the idea (it doesn't get dismissed or suppressed, which reduces the urgency around it), it externalizes it (removing the working memory cost of trying to hold it while also finishing the current work), and it separates idea capture from idea evaluation (the parking lot is not the place where ideas get acted on — that happens in a separate review). A weekly review of the parking lot — ideally during the weekly reset — processes accumulated ideas: discarding the ones that don't hold up on reflection, identifying the ones worth pursuing, and moving actionable ones into the actual task system. Project ideation space For high-IQ ADHD adults who generate significant volumes of project ideas and creative directions, a dedicated ideation space — a separate notebook, document, or physical board — provides a home for ideas that are too developed to be parking-lot items but not ready to become active projects. This is the holding area between interesting idea and actual commitment — a space that allows ideas to be developed and evaluated without being mistaken for current priorities. The ideation space prevents two common ADHD patterns: losing good ideas because they were never captured properly, and acting on every interesting idea immediately at the cost of finishing what is already in progress. Both patterns are expensive. The ideation space addresses both by giving ideas a real home without giving them premature execution priority. Principle 3: Use Novelty Intentionally Novelty is the ADHD brain's primary fuel, and treating it as a problem to be suppressed is both futile and wasteful. The more useful approach is using novelty deliberately — designing work and work environments so that novelty is applied strategically rather than allowed to drive behavior randomly. Rotate working environments The same desk, the same chair, the same view, the same ambient conditions — for an ADHD brain, environmental sameness is a slow drain on activation. Rotating working environments intentionally — between home desk, coffee shop, library, different rooms, outdoor spaces —

provides the low-level environmental novelty that the ADHD system uses as activation without introducing the high-level task novelty that derails focus. This is not instability. It is environmental design that accounts for how this specific brain uses environmental input. Many high-IQ ADHD adults do their most reliable deep work not at a dedicated desk but in a rotation of environments that each carry enough novelty to support engagement. Introduce intentional task variation For recurring tasks that lose activation over time due to familiarity, intentional variation restores novelty without changing the substance of the work. A different format for a recurring report. A different approach to a familiar problem. A constraint that requires a new solution. A timer-based challenge replacing an open-ended session. These variations are not inefficient detours. They are deliberate activation strategies for tasks that have crossed below the ADHD engagement threshold. The substance of the work remains the same. The activation profile changes enough to re-engage the system. Use the learning curve The period of steepest learning in any new domain — the phase when everything is unfamiliar and understanding is expanding rapidly — is among the highest-activation periods for the ADHD brain. Novel information, rapid skill acquisition, the challenge of genuine unfamiliarity — these are exactly the conditions the interest-based nervous system responds to most powerfully. High-IQ ADHD adults can use this deliberately: scheduling intensive learning phases for domains relevant to current projects, using the acquisition of new knowledge as the activation source for adjacent work, and positioning the unfamiliar aspects of a project as the focal point rather than the edge case. This is working with the novelty-seeking architecture rather than against it — using the brain's strongest activation response to propel real work rather than trying to suppress the response and proceeding on willpower. Principle 4: Make Boring Tasks Smaller, Stranger, or More Game-Like Not all tasks can be made interesting. Many of the tasks most necessary for adult functioning — administrative work, email management, financial maintenance, routine correspondence — are

genuinely, thoroughly low-interest for the high-IQ ADHD brain. No reframe makes them fascinating. The task is what it is. For these tasks, the strategy is not to manufacture interest where none exists. It is to change the activation profile through other mechanisms. Make it smaller A task that is genuinely uninteresting and also large is the worst possible combination for ADHD initiation. Breaking it into the smallest possible components — not as a preliminary step before the real work, but as the actual task structure — reduces the activation cost per unit and provides the completion dopamine that the ADHD reward system relies on. The question is not how can I tackle this project? but what is the smallest possible piece of this project that I can complete in the next fifteen minutes? Then that. Then another piece. Then another. Each completed piece provides a small reward signal. The accumulation of completed pieces produces the project. The individual pieces are manageable in a way the undivided project is not. Make it stranger Artificial constraints — arbitrary rules imposed on a boring task that have nothing to do with the task's substance — change the task's activation profile by introducing a problem-solving dimension that wasn't originally present. Write the first draft in exactly thirty sentences. Process email only in alphabetical order of sender. Complete the administrative task without using the mouse. Time the task and try to beat the previous time. These constraints are objectively irrational. They are also genuinely useful: they convert a flat, low-stimulation task into something that has a game structure, a challenge, and a slightly absurd novelty that the ADHD brain registers as more interesting than the unmodified version. High-IQ ADHD adults often resist this strategy initially because it seems intellectually undignified. The resistance softens when the task actually gets done, which it often doesn't without the constraint.

Make it a game

Gamification — building point systems, challenges, streaks, and reward structures around

necessary tasks — activates the ADHD reward system in a way that the intrinsic rewards of administrative task completion do not. Effective gamification for ADHD is personal rather than app-based. An app that gamifies productivity adds a layer of system complexity and becomes, itself, a potential distraction. A simple personal system — marks on a paper tracker, a visible streak counter, a small non-food reward after a block of low-interest tasks — is less elaborate and often more durable. The gamification doesn't need to be sophisticated. It needs to provide a reward signal that the task itself doesn't generate — something concrete and immediate that the ADHD brain can orient toward when the task's own reward is entirely abstract and delayed. Pair it with something enjoyable Temptation bundling — pairing a necessary but unengaging task with something genuinely enjoyable — is a well-researched behavioral strategy with specific relevance to ADHD. Research by Katherine Milkman and colleagues has demonstrated that pairing difficult tasks with enjoyable experiences significantly increases follow-through. For ADHD adults: listen to a specifically designated podcast or playlist only while doing administrative tasks. Work in a particularly appealing coffee shop specifically for low-interest work. Allow a specific enjoyable beverage specifically during focus blocks for unengaging tasks. The enjoyable element doesn't remove the activation challenge — but it changes the reward profile of beginning, which reduces the initiation cost. The designated pairing matters. The enjoyable element that is available everywhere and at all times doesn't work as a temptation bundle — it doesn't provide differential activation for the specific task. The enjoyable element that is strictly paired with the specific unengaging task provides both a reward signal for engagement and a mild urgency to begin. Principle 5: Protect Deep Work Windows Deep work — extended, focused engagement with cognitively demanding material — is both the high-IQ ADHD brain's greatest potential output and one of its most fragile states to maintain. When conditions are right — the environment is appropriately stimulating, the task is engaging, interruptions are absent, and the activation state is high — high-IQ ADHD adults can produce work of exceptional quality and depth. The challenge is creating and protecting the conditions that allow this state to develop and persist.

Identify your peak activation window

The ADHD brain's activation state varies across the day in patterns that are individual but consistent. Most people have a period of two to four hours during which executive function, focus capacity, and creative output are at their daily peak. Identifying this window — through deliberate observation of energy, focus quality, and output across several weeks — allows it to be protected for the most cognitively demanding work. For many people this window is mid-to-late morning. For ADHD adults with delayed sleep phase, it may be later — early afternoon, or even late evening. The specific timing is less important than the identification and protection of the window. Defend the window against lower-priority demand The peak activation window is the most valuable cognitive resource in the day. It should be defended deliberately against email, administrative tasks, routine communications, and meetings that could happen at other times. These lower-activation activities consume the same window that deep work requires, and they don't need the peak window the way deep work does. This requires boundary-setting with the environment — not scheduling meetings during the peak window where possible, not beginning the day with email, structuring external demands around the window rather than in it. This is not always fully achievable in all professional contexts. But the principle — protecting peak activation time for peak-activation work — is worth implementing as completely as circumstances allow. Pre-load the deep work session Beginning a deep work session requires activation that may not be available at the moment of sitting down. Pre-loading — a brief ritual that activates the system before the deep work begins — reduces the lag between sitting down and productive engagement. Effective pre-loading activities for deep ADHD brains include: brief aerobic movement (five to ten minutes of brisk walking or exercise), reviewing the specific question or problem the session will address, reading two or three minutes of relevant material to prime the domain, or — for some people — a brief period of deliberately engaging music that transitions to the working sound environment as the session begins. The pre-load is not procrastination. It is activation engineering — using the ADHD motivational system's responsiveness to specific inputs to enter the deep work state more quickly than cold initiation allows.

Use transition rituals to close sessions

Ending a deep work session is as important as beginning one, and for ADHD brains the transition out of a focused state can be as challenging as the transition in. Without a deliberate closing ritual, sessions either overrun (hyperfocus) or end abruptly without capturing where the work is and what the next action is — making resumption harder. A brief closing ritual at the end of each deep work session — three to five minutes, always the same — includes: noting exactly where in the task you are, writing the specific next action, and transitioning the active document or workspace to its resumed position. This closing note is the re-entry point for the next session, providing the orientation that reduces the activation cost of resumption. Hemingway's practice of stopping mid-sentence at the end of a writing session — leaving himself an easy re-entry point — is a well-known version of this. The principle applies broadly: leave the door open and clearly marked for the next entry. Principle 6: Manage Energy, Not Just Time Standard time management assumes that an hour is an hour — that the same unit of scheduled time will produce approximately the same output regardless of when in the day it occurs or what cognitive state precedes it. For ADHD adults, this assumption is particularly wrong. Output depends not just on available time but on available cognitive energy — the activation state that determines whether the ADHD system can access focus, creativity, and executive function in a given window. A low-activation hour produces less than a high-activation fifteen minutes. Managing the day around energy states — rather than treating all time as equivalent — is a more accurate model of how this brain actually functions. Map your energy landscape A useful exercise: for one to two weeks, note energy and focus quality at several points throughout each day — morning, late morning, early afternoon, mid-afternoon, evening. Simple ratings are sufficient: high, medium, low, or a number scale. After two weeks, patterns emerge. The natural rhythm of daily energy — when activation is reliably high, when it reliably drops, when a second wind typically arrives — becomes visible as a pattern rather than random variation. This map is individual. It is also actionable: align high-activation windows with high-activation tasks, and low-activation windows with low-activation ones.

Match tasks to energy states

The practical application of the energy landscape is simple in principle and requires deliberate scheduling discipline in practice:

  • High-activation windows → deep work, creative production, complex analysis, difficult conversations, strategic thinking
  • Medium-activation windows → meetings, collaborative work, email and communication, lighter cognitive tasks
  • Low-activation windows → administrative tasks, filing, simple correspondence, physical organization, routine maintenance

This alignment means that the cognitively demanding work happens when the cognitive resources to support it are available, and the low-demand work happens when they aren't — rather than the common ADHD pattern of low-demand tasks filling high-activation time (because they feel manageable to start) while high-demand tasks are attempted during low-activation periods (because urgency forces them there regardless of energy). Plan recovery as deliberately as work High-output work — particularly deep, creative, or emotionally demanding work — depletes cognitive resources that require real recovery. For ADHD adults who push hard during high-activation periods, recovery is not optional downtime. It is the restoration of the resource that makes the next high-activation period possible. Recovery activities that genuinely restore cognitive resources are those that do not demand executive function or sustained attention: physical movement, time in nature, genuinely restful leisure, social connection that is low-demand and pleasurable. Scrolling social media — the default recovery choice for many people — does not restore cognitive resources. It consumes low-level attention without providing genuine rest. Treating it as recovery produces the experience of having rested without actually having done so. Scheduled, deliberate recovery — built into the day as explicitly as work blocks, treated as non-negotiable rather than as time available for overflow tasks — is a legitimate and important productivity strategy. It is also, for high-achieving ADHD adults who feel guilty about rest, among the most difficult strategies to actually implement. Principle 7: Use Deadlines Carefully Deadlines are the most reliable activation tool available to the ADHD brain. They are also, if used without design, a source of chronic stress, reactive rather than proactive work, and the compounding cost of a life managed entirely in crisis mode.

The goal is not to eliminate deadline-driven productivity — that would be removing the most reliable activation mechanism available. The goal is to use deadlines deliberately rather than experiencing them as the only force that produces movement. Create earlier artificial deadlines The most straightforward way to use deadlines more intentionally is to create earlier ones. A project due to a client on Friday has a personal deadline of Wednesday. A draft due to an editor in two weeks has an internal review deadline in ten days. An important email due before end of week is treated as due before noon today. The effectiveness of an artificial deadline depends on the consequence for missing it being real enough to activate the system. A consequence that exists only as internal disappointment is often insufficient — the ADHD brain accurately models its own willingness to accept that consequence without full activation. A consequence that involves another person — a partner who is expecting the draft, a coach who will ask about it, a public commitment — is neurologically more similar to a real deadline. Commitment devices A commitment device is a structure set up in advance that makes following through easier and not following through more costly — removing the option of convenient non-compliance in the future by making a binding commitment in the present, when intention is high. Effective commitment devices for ADHD include: scheduling a specific appointment to deliver the work (the appointment makes the deadline concrete and social); making a public statement about a goal that creates social accountability; using a service like Beeminder that imposes financial consequences for goal non-completion; establishing a working arrangement with a body-double partner who is expecting deliverables. The key is making the future consequences real enough that the ADHD motivational system treats them as approaching urgency rather than abstract future obligation. Build deadline buffers systematically For externally imposed deadlines, the standard practice should be to treat the real deadline as several days earlier than it actually is — and to maintain this buffer despite the cognitive tendency to use the awareness of the real deadline as permission to use the buffer.

Building this buffer requires the weekly reset discipline of reviewing upcoming deadlines and establishing the personal deadline that precedes them. The buffer is not for leisurely progress. It is insurance against the ADHD reality that tasks expand, transitions cost time, other things break, and the sprint to the finish line may need to start earlier than the brain naturally initiates it. Principle 8: Avoid Over-Engineering the System This principle is last because it is both important and specifically resistant to uptake by the high-IQ ADHD brain. System design is interesting. Using systems is routine. The ADHD brain will reliably prefer the former to the latter, and will consistently find reasons why the current system needs revision, expansion, or replacement before it can be properly used. The over-engineered system — the elaborate productivity architecture with multiple interdependent components, sophisticated categorization schemes, and frequent maintenance requirements — fails not because it is poorly designed but because it is too interesting to use and too complex to maintain when activation is low. The system that works for ADHD is characteristically simpler than the system the ADHD brain wants to build. Practical tests for system over-engineering: Can you use this when your executive function is at 40%? If the system requires high executive engagement to use, it won't be used on the days when executive function is most depleted — which are exactly the days when the system is most needed. Does maintaining this system take more time than the work it manages? A system that requires more overhead than it saves is a net cost, regardless of its elegance. Have you been spending more time designing it than using it? If the answer is yes, the system is serving the design interest rather than the productivity function. Shipping the imperfect system and using it is better than continuing to refine the perfect one that never gets deployed. Would a simpler version serve the actual need? Almost always, the answer is yes. The simpler version may be less satisfying to design. It is more likely to be used. The minimum viable productivity system — the simplest version that captures tasks, manages time, and supports initiation — is more valuable than the most sophisticated version that sits admired and unused. Shipping it, using it imperfectly, and improving it incrementally based on actual use is the correct sequence. Build to use. Not to admire.

Integrating the Principles

These principles are not a system in themselves. They are design criteria — ways of evaluating strategies and systems to determine whether they are built for this specific brain or for a different one. A strategy that is flexible rather than perfect, separates capture from execution, uses novelty intentionally, changes the activation profile of boring tasks, protects deep work, matches tasks to energy, uses deadlines deliberately, and resists over-engineering — this is a strategy built for the high-IQ ADHD operating system described in Chapter 11. None of these principles are natural or automatic. They require design, trial, and adjustment. They will fail in their specific implementations and need to be revised. That is not evidence of failure. It is the normal process of building a functional system for an atypical brain in a world that wasn't designed for it. The brain you have is capable of extraordinary output. The system you need is the one that works for that brain — not the one that looks most impressive on paper, not the one that would work for someone with a different neurological architecture, but the one that accounts honestly for the way this specific operating system actually runs. Build that system. Then use it. Then rebuild the parts that don't work. That iteration is not a failure cycle. It is engineering.
