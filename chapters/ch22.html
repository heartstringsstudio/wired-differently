Chapter 22

Self-Assessment Reflection Questions

Not a Test — A Conversation With Yourself

The questions in this chapter are not diagnostic instruments. They will not tell you whether you have ADHD, confirm a diagnosis, or produce a score that means anything clinical. If you need that — and if this manual has resonated with you in ways that suggest you might — Chapter 9 describes what a proper clinical evaluation looks like and how to access one. What these questions do is something different and, in some respects, more personally demanding. They invite honest, specific self-examination of the patterns that this manual has described — not in the abstract, but in your own life, your own history, and your own daily experience. They are the questions that, answered honestly over time, produce the self-knowledge on which every practical strategy in this manual depends. The word honestly is doing significant work in that sentence. The high-IQ ADHD adult who reads these questions may find themselves performing honesty rather than practicing it — generating sophisticated, self-aware-sounding answers that are actually a form of self-protection. The answers that are most useful are not the most articulate or the most intellectually interesting. They are the most accurate. Take your time with these. A few questions at a sitting, returned to over days or weeks, is more productive than answering all of them in a single session in which the later answers are exhausted approximations of the earlier ones. Write the answers down where possible — the act of writing externalizes the thinking in ways that often reveal gaps and clarifications that purely mental processing doesn't. These questions are organized by domain. Start where resonance is strongest. Section 1: History and Patterns These questions explore the long view — the patterns that have repeated across years and decades, the history that provides the clearest evidence about what is actually characteristic rather than situational. 1. When you look back at your educational experience, what is the pattern?

Not the overall summary — I did well in school or I struggled — but the specific texture. Which subjects engaged you and which didn't, and what was the difference? Which teachers do you remember, and why? When did you do your best work, and what was present in those circumstances that wasn't present in others? When did you fall behind, and what specifically was happening then? If you can access school records or reports — or remember specific teacher comments — what language was used? Could do better. Doesn't apply himself. Underachieves. Easily distracted. Brilliant when engaged. Needs to focus. These recurring phrases are a developmental history. 2. What is the trail of unfinished things in your life? Not a self-critical inventory — a factual one. Projects started with genuine enthusiasm and abandoned. Hobbies pursued intensely and then dropped. Creative work that reached a stage of near-completion and stalled. Relationships that began with full engagement and then something shifted. Courses, certifications, degrees, business ideas, creative projects — what is the specific shape of the unfinished landscape? What do the unfinished things have in common? At what stage did most of them stall? Was it the beginning — never quite launched? The middle — started but the initial excitement depleted? The end — most of the way there but the last 20% never quite done? 3. What does your history of employment look like — and what does the texture tell you? Not the formal narrative — the actual experience. Which roles energized you and which depleted you, and what specifically was different? Where did you thrive and where did you consistently underperform? What feedback have you received repeatedly across different jobs, from different managers, in different contexts? What has been praised consistently and what has been a persistent concern? If you've moved between roles or organizations more than once, what drove each departure? Boredom? Conflict? Performance issues? Structural mismatch? The pattern of departures is as informative as the pattern of positions. 4. What have been the recurring failure patterns across your adult life? Not the specific failures — the patterns. The same type of thing going wrong in different contexts. Relationships affected by the same dynamic. Professional situations that have produced the same outcome despite different environments. Financial patterns that recur

despite different incomes. Organizational challenges that reappear in each new home, new job, new life configuration. The recurrence across contexts is the signal. A failure in one context is situational. A failure that reappears in multiple different contexts, across years, despite genuine effort and different circumstances, is structural. 5. What has effort looked like for you, compared to what your outcomes suggest? This is the question that the lazy myth distorts most directly. If you compare the effort you've expended — the compensatory work, the all-nighters, the elaborate systems, the sustained anxiety management, the crisis-mode productivity — to the outcomes that effort has produced, what is the ratio? Are you producing outcomes that seem to require the effort you're spending? Or does it take significantly more effort than it appears to, from outside, to produce what you produce? The effort-to-outcome ratio is often the clearest personal evidence for or against the compensation cost described throughout this manual. Section 2: Attention and Activation 6. When are you fully present, and when are you not? Think about the specific conditions under which you are genuinely, completely engaged with what is in front of you — where the attention is full, the thinking is clear, and the performance reflects your actual capacity. What is present in those conditions? What is the content, the environment, the level of challenge, the stakes, the novelty? Now think about the conditions in which you are technically present but not actually there — where attention is drifting, the mind is elsewhere, and you are performing the appearance of engagement rather than having it. What is specific and consistent about those conditions? The gap between these two states — and what produces it — is the most informative single picture of your attention regulation pattern. 7. What happens when you try to do something you know you should do but don't want to do?

Not the outcome — what actually happens, in real time, in the body and the mind? Is there a specific feeling — a kind of wall, a blankness, an active aversion, a restless pull toward something else? How long does it last? What, if anything, breaks it? Does urgency break it? Does another person's presence break it? Does the right music or environment change it? The phenomenology of initiation failure — the specific felt experience of not being able to start something you intend to start — is one of the most diagnostically informative self-observations available. 8. What is your relationship with the things on your to-do list? How many items have been on the list for more than a week? More than a month? More than a year? Not the genuinely lower-priority items — the ones that keep appearing because they are actually important but consistently resist being done despite that importance? What specifically is different about those items compared to the ones that get done? 9. How do you experience time? What happens when you sit down to work on something and the next time you check it has been two hours? What happens when you're certain five minutes have passed and it has been thirty? How often are you surprised by how little time remains before a deadline or commitment that you knew about? How often do tasks take significantly longer than you estimated, consistently, across different types of tasks? How do you feel about the future — specifically, how real do events and deadlines that are two weeks away feel right now, compared to events and deadlines that are tomorrow? 10. What is the specific experience of hyperfocus for you? Can you describe a recent hyperfocus episode — what triggered it, what the experience was like during it, how long it lasted, and how it ended? What were you unable to do during it that you would otherwise have been able to do — eat, drink, respond to a message, notice the time? How voluntary was the hyperfocus? Could you have chosen not to enter it? Could you have chosen to leave it before it resolved naturally? What does this tell you about how much control you have over your own attention direction?

Section 3: Executive Function

11. What are the tasks in your life that feel strangely, disproportionately difficult? Not difficult because they are complex or require skills you don't have — difficult in a way that seems out of proportion to what they actually require. The simple email that takes three days to send. The phone call that needs to be made and doesn't get made for a week. The administrative task that a reasonable estimate suggests should take fifteen minutes and that sits undone for a month. What do these tasks have in common? Are they low-interest? Do they have ambiguous starting points? Do they require initiation without urgency? Do they involve a risk of evaluation or rejection? The pattern in what feels strangely difficult is more informative than any individual item. 12. How does your physical space reflect your executive function? Not how it looks on the days you've cleaned it in preparation for company — how it looks on an ordinary Tuesday. What is the relationship between the organizational systems you have and the actual state of your space? Have you designed systems that work for a few weeks and then stop being used? Are there areas of particular entropy that recur despite repeated attempts to address them? What do you know about why those areas are consistently most resistant? 13. How do you manage money, and what does the pattern show? Again, not the formal summary — the actual pattern. Bills paid late not because of lack of funds but because of administrative initiation failure. Subscriptions that have been running for months past the intention to cancel them. Financial planning that is understood in the abstract and perpetually not implemented in the specific. Purchases made impulsively that were regretted — not occasionally, but as a pattern. What is the gap between your financial intentions and your financial reality, and what specifically produces that gap? 14. When you make a commitment, what is the actual probability of follow-through — and what determines it? Not the intended probability — the actual historical rate. What types of commitments do you reliably keep and what types do you reliably struggle with? What conditions increase follow-through: external accountability, urgency, importance to someone you care about, a

written record? What conditions decrease it: low urgency, no external accountability, a task that requires multiple steps, a commitment made in a high-enthusiasm moment that has since depleted? The honest answer to this question is the starting point for building commitments and accountability structures that actually work for you. 15. How do you make decisions, and what does that cost you? What happens when you need to make a significant decision — how long does it take, how much analysis does it involve, at what point does the analysis become unproductive rather than useful? What decisions have you been putting off making, and for how long? What do those decisions have in common? What is the relationship between how much you analyze a decision and how satisfied you are with the outcome? Is more analysis producing better decisions, or is it primarily delaying them? Section 4: Emotional Patterns 16. What is your relationship with criticism and negative evaluation? Not the philosophical position — the actual felt experience. When someone criticizes your work, your behavior, or your choices — even mildly and constructively — what happens internally? How long does the response last? What does the internal processing of criticism look like over the hours and days following it? What do you do to avoid situations in which criticism is possible? What opportunities, relationships, or experiences have you not pursued because the possibility of negative evaluation was too costly? The avoidance pattern is often more informative than the response to criticism that actually occurs. 17. How intense are your emotional responses, relative to the situations that produce them? Think of specific recent examples — a response that you recognized as disproportionate either during or shortly after having it. What was the trigger? What was the response? What was the gap between the trigger's apparent significance and the response's actual intensity?

How quickly do your emotions escalate from mild to significant? How long do they typically take to return to baseline? What, if anything, shortens the return — and what extends it? 18. What is the quality of your internal self-talk when things go wrong? When you make a mistake, miss a commitment, fall short of your own expectations, or produce work that doesn't meet your standard — what do you say to yourself about it? Is the self-talk proportionate to what happened, or does it escalate beyond the specific incident into broader characterizations? Would you apply the same internal language to a close friend in the same situation? The gap between how you would speak to a friend and how you speak to yourself in moments of failure is a direct measure of the shame narrative's presence and intensity. 19. What are the situations and relationships where you feel most emotionally safe — and what do they have in common? Where do you not have to perform? Where do you feel genuinely accepted rather than provisionally tolerated? Where can you be inconsistent, imperfect, and irregular without experiencing the social and relational costs those qualities produce in other contexts? The characteristics of these environments and relationships describe what emotional safety requires for you — and the contrast with environments that don't provide it illustrates the cost of its absence. 20. Where in your life is shame most present, and in what form does it appear? Shame often doesn't announce itself as shame. It appears as avoidance — not pursuing something that matters because failure would be too costly. As deflection — changing the subject when a vulnerability area arises. As aggression — responding sharply to indirect challenges to the areas of most vulnerability. As minimization — it's not a big deal about things that clearly are. Where are the areas of your life most strongly defended? What is being protected there?

Section 5: Compensation and Masking

21. Where do you compensate, and what does it cost? What specifically do you do, systematically, to appear more organized, more attentive, more consistent, or more neurotypically functional than you actually are? What do other people see that is different from what is happening internally? What effort is behind performances that look effortless? This question requires honesty about the gap between presentation and experience — the work behind the appearance of competence that no one sees and that isn't accounted for in anyone's assessment of what you can manage. 22. What do you do in professional settings that you wouldn't need to do if your brain worked differently? The specific compensatory behaviors — arriving early to buffer time blindness, preparing for conversations in unusual depth because attention drift makes improvisation risky, reading everything twice because the first pass didn't fully register, repeating information back immediately to consolidate it against working memory loss — what is on your specific list? These behaviors are data. They describe, in functional terms, what your brain actually needs to compensate for in order to produce the performance it produces. 23. When does the mask slip, and what happens when it does? When are you least able to maintain the performance of neurotypical competence? Late in the day? After sustained social performance? Under particular types of stress? In specific types of interactions? With specific people? What is different about those moments — in the internal experience, and in the external behavior? What do people see when the compensatory performance is unavailable? 24. What would your functioning look like without the compensatory strategies you've developed? This is a thought experiment rather than a memory. If you removed the workarounds — the elaborate reminders, the strategic early arrivals, the covering all-nighters, the verbal fluency that masks attention drift — what would remain? What would be visible that is currently invisible? This question points toward the actual impairment that the compensatory system is managing — and therefore toward the specific supports that would most directly address what the compensation is covering.

Section 6: Environments and Systems

25. What environments bring out your best functioning — and what do they have in common? Think specifically. The physical spaces, the social configurations, the levels of ambient stimulation, the presence or absence of time pressure, the degree of novelty or familiarity, the types of people around you, the nature of the work you're doing. When have you performed at your genuine ceiling, and what was surrounding you? The pattern in your best-functioning environments is the specification for the conditions your brain actually needs — and the gap between those conditions and your current default environment is a map of the modifications most likely to improve daily functioning. 26. What systems have you built that worked and then stopped working — and what does the pattern reveal? Not a general reference to systems that failed — specific systems, with the timeline and the failure mechanism. The planner that was used diligently for three weeks. The filing system that maintained itself for a month. The morning routine that held for two weeks. The organizational app that became a repository of tasks that were logged but not reviewed. At what point did each stop working? What was happening in the broader context at that point? Was it the loss of novelty? An external disruption that broke the chain? An increase in life complexity that the system couldn't absorb? The system becoming too elaborate to maintain? The failure mechanisms describe the specific vulnerabilities that the next system design needs to address. 27. What would your ideal working day look like if you designed it from scratch, with full self-knowledge? Not the idealized day of maximum output and perfect discipline — the realistic ideal, designed for how your brain actually operates. When would it start? What would the first hour involve? When would the most demanding cognitive work happen? How would transitions be managed? What would be in the environment? Who, if anyone, would be present? How much variety and how much structure? When would genuine rest occur?

The gap between this ideal and your current daily reality describes the environmental and structural modifications most worth pursuing. Section 7: Treatment and Support 28. What support do you currently have, and is it adequate? An honest inventory: medication (what, when, at what dose, how consistently taken, how well it is working)? Therapy (type, frequency, how ADHD-informed the therapist is)? Coaching? Workplace accommodations? Partner or family understanding and support? Self-management strategies that are actually being used? For each element of support that exists, is it adequate — is it actually addressing the specific impairments it is intended to address? For each element that is absent, what would be the most material improvement that its addition would produce? 29. What has stopped you from seeking or maintaining support in the past? Cost? Access? The belief that your situation doesn't warrant it? The belief that you should be able to manage without it? Shame about needing support? A previous negative experience with a clinician or therapist who didn't understand the profile? The executive function impairment that makes making and keeping appointments itself a barrier? The specific barriers to support-seeking are more actionable than the general awareness that support would be useful. Each barrier has a specific response — financial barriers have specific solutions, access barriers have specific solutions, shame barriers have specific responses — but only when the barrier is identified specifically rather than experienced generally. 30. What does help actually look like for you — not in the abstract, but specifically? This is perhaps the most practically important question in the chapter. Not what kind of help do people with ADHD need? — but what specific things, when provided, actually make a material difference to my functioning? The answer may include things that don't appear in standard ADHD management literature. It may include specific people whose presence activates your functioning. Specific environmental conditions that your brain requires. Specific types of accountability that work better than others. Specific communication formats that produce better results than others. Specific times of day or week when you are most and least receptive to different types of support.

The specificity of what actually helps — developed through honest reflection on what has and hasn't worked across the full history of your attempts to manage — is the most valuable information you can bring to conversations with clinicians, coaches, partners, and employers. Using Your Answers These questions do not produce a conclusion. They produce material — the specific, honest, personal data that makes every other element of this manual more applicable to the actual person reading it rather than the generic profile it describes. The most useful thing to do with honest answers to these questions is not to analyze them further. It is to bring them — to a clinician conducting an evaluation, to a therapist exploring the psychological landscape, to a coach developing practical strategies, to a partner trying to understand, or simply to yourself as the foundation of a more accurate self-story than the one that the years of misunderstanding produced. The questions are not the work. They are the preparation for it. The work is everything else in this manual, applied to the specific brain that your honest answers have described.
