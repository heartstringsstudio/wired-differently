Chapter 26

Resources

Where to Go Next

This chapter is a curated guide to the resources most useful for high-IQ adults with ADHD — not a comprehensive bibliography, but a selective, annotated collection organized by what you're looking for and why each resource is worth your time. Each resource is described with enough context to evaluate its relevance to your specific situation. The descriptions are honest: where a resource has limitations, those are noted alongside its strengths. Where a resource has been updated or may have changed since this writing, verification of current availability is recommended. A note on quality: the internet contains vast quantities of ADHD content of widely varying reliability. Social media, in particular, has produced a wave of ADHD content that is engaging, validating, and frequently inaccurate or misleading in its clinical claims. The resources listed here are grounded in research, developed by clinicians with established expertise, or produced by organizations with rigorous quality standards. They are not the most entertaining resources. They are the most useful ones. Section 1: Organizations These are the primary professional and advocacy organizations with the most reliable, research-grounded information about ADHD. All maintain websites with educational content, professional directories, and evidence-based guidance. CHADD — Children and Adults with Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder chadd.org CHADD is the largest nonprofit ADHD organization in the United States and one of the most comprehensive sources of evidence-based ADHD information available online. Originally focused on children, the organization has substantially expanded its adult ADHD resources over recent years. Most useful for: Locating a CHADD-affiliated professional in your area, accessing the National Resource Center on ADHD (a program of CHADD funded by the CDC), finding local support groups, and reading research-grounded educational articles on adult ADHD topics.

The website's adult section — including the National Resource Center — is specifically worth bookmarking. It provides evidence-based information on diagnosis, treatment, relationships, workplace issues, and more, reviewed by clinical experts. ADDA — Attention Deficit Disorder Association add.org ADDA is specifically focused on adults with ADHD, distinguishing it from CHADD's broader lifespan focus. Its resources, webinars, and community are oriented toward the specific challenges and contexts of adult life with ADHD — careers, relationships, finances, health, and self-management. Most useful for: Adults who find CHADD's broader focus less directly applicable; anyone seeking adult-specific ADHD community, peer support, and professional development resources. ADDA also provides a professional directory and runs the ADDA Virtual Peer Support Groups, which are free or low-cost and widely accessible. NIMH — National Institute of Mental Health nimh.nih.gov/health/topics/attention-deficit-hyperactivity-disorder-adhd The National Institute of Mental Health provides federally funded, scientifically reviewed information on ADHD for general audiences. Its ADHD pages offer reliable overview information, current research summaries, and guidance on finding help. Most useful for: Accurate, government-reviewed foundational information on ADHD; research updates from federally funded studies; links to clinical trials for those interested in participation. Less detailed on practical management strategies than the advocacy organizations, but authoritative on the clinical and scientific dimensions. CDC — Centers for Disease Control and Prevention cdc.gov/adhd The CDC's ADHD pages provide population-level data on ADHD prevalence, diagnosis rates, treatment rates, and outcomes. They also offer educational content reviewed for scientific accuracy. Most useful for: Statistical and epidemiological information; understanding ADHD at the population level; accessing the research summaries that form the basis for many clinical guidelines. The CDC's ADHD pages are particularly useful for family members, educators, and employers seeking to understand the scope and nature of the condition.

APSARD — The American Professional Society of ADHD and Related Disorders apsard.org APSARD is a professional society of clinicians and researchers specializing in ADHD. Its website includes a professional directory specifically searchable for clinicians with ADHD expertise — particularly useful for locating evaluators and treatment providers who specialize in adult ADHD. Most useful for: Finding qualified ADHD specialists, particularly for complex or twice-exceptional presentations. The directory allows filtering by location and specialty, providing a more targeted starting point than general therapy directories. ADDitude Magazine additudemag.com ADDitude is the most widely read consumer publication on ADHD, with a substantial online archive of articles, webinars, and expert Q&As on ADHD management. It bridges clinical information and practical guidance in accessible language. Most useful for: Practical, daily-life-applicable strategies; first-person perspectives from adults with ADHD; accessible explanations of research findings. Quality varies more than the professional organizations' publications — some content is excellent, some is more anecdotal. Best used as a starting point for topics to explore more rigorously, rather than as a primary source for clinical decisions. The website's webinar archive — covering topics from medication to relationships to workplace strategies, many presented by recognized ADHD experts — is a particularly valuable free resource. ADHD Coaches Organization (ACO) adhdcoaches.org The ACO is the professional organization for ADHD coaches, providing training standards, a code of ethics, and a professional directory. For adults seeking ADHD coaching — described in Chapter 10 — the ACO directory is the most reliable starting point for finding qualified practitioners. Most useful for: Locating trained ADHD coaches; understanding what ADHD coaching is and how it differs from therapy; assessing coach credentials and training background. The directory allows filtering by location, delivery method (in-person versus virtual), and population specialty.

Section 2: Books

The books listed here represent the most rigorously grounded, clinically useful, and intellectually honest resources available for high-IQ adults with ADHD. Each is annotated with its specific value and appropriate audience. On ADHD: Clinical and Scientific Foundation Taking Charge of Adult ADHD — Russell A. Barkley, PhD (2010, Guilford Press) The most comprehensive, research-grounded practical guide to adult ADHD available in book form. Barkley is the preeminent researcher in the field, and this book represents the translation of decades of research into accessible, actionable guidance for adults with ADHD. Most useful for: Adults seeking the most rigorously evidence-based practical guidance available. The book covers diagnosis, the neurological basis of ADHD, treatment options, and a comprehensive set of management strategies across life domains. Less focused on the high-IQ profile specifically, but foundational for understanding the condition accurately. ADHD and the Nature of Self-Control — Russell A. Barkley, PhD (1997, Guilford Press) A more technical and theoretical work than Taking Charge, this book presents Barkley's comprehensive model of ADHD as a disorder of behavioral inhibition and self-regulation. It is more academic than the practical guides but provides the most rigorous conceptual framework for understanding why ADHD produces the specific impairments it does. Most useful for: Adults who want a deep, research-grounded understanding of the neurological and cognitive architecture of ADHD — particularly those who find the operating system framework in Chapter 11 of this manual useful and want to explore it further. Not a practical management guide; an intellectual foundation. Driven to Distraction: Recognizing and Coping with Attention Deficit Disorder — Edward M. Hallowell, MD, and John J. Ratey, MD (Revised edition, 2011, Anchor Books) The book that introduced adult ADHD to a wide general audience when first published in 1994. Updated and still widely read, it combines clinical insight with accessible, humanistic writing and first-person accounts. Hallowell and Ratey are both clinicians with ADHD themselves, which shapes the book's perspective. Most useful for: Adults newly encountering the concept of adult ADHD who want an approachable, narrative-rich introduction before moving to more technical resources. Less

rigorously structured than Barkley's work but warmer and more personally accessible. Good starting point; complements rather than replaces more systematic resources. Smart But Stuck: Unraveling Hidden Intelligence and Leadership Potential — Thomas E. Brown, PhD (2014, Jossey-Bass) Brown's book is specifically focused on the profile described in this manual: high-IQ adults with ADHD who have been blocked by the condition despite genuine intellectual ability. It uses detailed case studies to illustrate the specific ways ADHD impairs high-achieving adults and explores the gap between cognitive ability and executive function performance. Most useful for: High-IQ ADHD adults who find generic ADHD resources insufficiently matched to their specific profile. The case studies often produce the specific recognition described in this manual's introduction — the experience of seeing one's own experience described accurately for the first time. ADHD: A Hunter in a Farmer's World — Thom Hartmann (4th edition, 2019, Healing Arts Press) Hartmann's metaphorical framework — ADHD as a hunter's brain operating in an environment built for farmers — is not a scientific model but an accessible reframe that many readers find useful for understanding the environmental mismatch between ADHD neurological characteristics and conventional institutional expectations. Most useful for: Adults who find the neurological framework accessible but want an additional intuitive metaphor for the environmental fit problem. Not a clinical resource; a conceptual one. Best read alongside the evidence-based texts rather than instead of them. On Twice-Exceptionality Twice-Exceptional Students: A Handbook for School Counselors — Edited by Joy Lawson Davis and Daphne Pereles (2014, Prufrock Press) Although written primarily for educational professionals, this handbook is one of the most comprehensive resources on twice-exceptionality available in accessible form. It covers identification, educational approaches, psychological support, and the specific profiles that emerge when giftedness coexists with ADHD, learning differences, and autism. Most useful for: Adults seeking a rigorous, research-grounded overview of twice-exceptionality; parents of twice-exceptional children; professionals working with this population.

The Gifted Adult: A Revolutionary Guide for Liberating Everyday Genius — Mary-Elaine Jacobsen, PsyD (1999, Ballantine Books) Jacobsen's book focuses on the psychological experience of high intellectual ability in adulthood — the specific challenges of intensity, complexity, and the mismatch between gifted adults and conventional environments — with considerable relevance to the twice-exceptional profile even where ADHD is not the primary focus. Most useful for: Adults who resonate with the giftedness dimension of the twice-exceptional profile and want to understand its specific psychological features and challenges more deeply. On Executive Function Smart but Scattered — Peg Dawson, EdD, and Richard Guare, PhD (2009, Guilford Press) A practical, accessible guide to understanding and building executive function skills. Originally written for children, the adult companion volume — Smart but Scattered — for Adults — is directly applicable to the ADHD adult population. Most useful for: Adults who want a systematic, practical approach to building executive function skills through external structures and deliberate practice. Highly compatible with the strategies in Chapters 12 and 13 of this manual. The Executive Function Guidebook — Roberta Kaufman, EdD (2019, Corwin) A practical, strategy-focused resource for building executive function skills in educational and professional contexts. More structured than Dawson and Guare, with specific tools for each executive function domain. Most useful for: Adults seeking structured, domain-specific strategies for executive function improvement; educators working with twice-exceptional students.

On Emotional Regulation and Related Topics

Scattered Minds: The Origins and Healing of Attention Deficit Disorder — Gabor Maté, MD (2019, Avery) Maté's book takes a developmental and trauma-informed perspective on ADHD, emphasizing the relationship between early attachment, stress, and the ADHD nervous system. It is not a standard clinical text and some of its claims are more speculative than the evidence fully supports, but it offers a perspective on ADHD's emotional and relational dimensions that is often absent from more neurologically focused resources. Most useful for: Adults whose ADHD experience is significantly intertwined with emotional history, attachment patterns, or trauma; those who find the purely neurological framing insufficient to capture their full experience. Best read critically and alongside more evidence-based texts. Self-Compassion: The Proven Power of Being Kind to Yourself — Kristin Neff, PhD (2011, William Morrow) Neff's foundational text on self-compassion research and practice is directly relevant to the shame, self-criticism, and imposter syndrome patterns described throughout this manual. The research base is robust, and the practical exercises are accessible. Most useful for: Adults who recognize that self-criticism is a significant feature of their ADHD experience and who want a structured, research-grounded approach to developing self-compassion. Among the most important books in this list for high-achieving ADHD adults whose primary suffering is shame rather than symptom severity. The Body Keeps the Score: Brain, Mind, and Body in the Healing of Trauma — Bessel van der Kolk, MD (2014, Viking) While not an ADHD resource per se, van der Kolk's comprehensive account of how stress and trauma are held in the nervous system is relevant for many ADHD adults — particularly those whose ADHD has produced significant emotional consequences, and those who suspect that trauma history interacts with their ADHD profile. Most useful for: Adults exploring the relationship between ADHD, stress, emotional regulation, and somatic experience; those working with a trauma-informed therapist.

On Productivity, Creativity, and Work

Spark: The Revolutionary New Science of Exercise and the Brain — John J. Ratey, MD, with Eric Hagerman (2008, Little, Brown) Ratey's account of the neurological effects of exercise on brain function — including attention, executive function, and mood — provides the scientific basis for exercise as a primary ADHD management strategy. Chapter 20 of this manual draws directly on his framework. Most useful for: Adults who need to understand the neurological mechanism behind exercise recommendations in order to be motivated by them; anyone who has dismissed exercise as a wellness cliché and would benefit from understanding it as a clinical intervention. The Big Leap: Conquer Your Hidden Fear and Take Life to the Next Level — Gay Hendricks, PhD (2009, HarperOne) Hendricks' concept of the Zone of Genius — the specific work that combines high competence with genuine activation — is referenced in both the career and productivity chapters of this manual. The book's framework for identifying and protecting this zone is practical and directly applicable to ADHD career design. Most useful for: Adults engaged in career design and the identification of work that activates their system rather than depleting it. Not an ADHD resource specifically, but one of the most useful career frameworks for the high-IQ ADHD adult. Deep Work: Rules for Focused Success in a Distracted World — Cal Newport (2016, Grand Central Publishing) Newport's systematic case for the value of sustained, uninterrupted cognitive work, and his practical strategies for protecting it, are directly applicable to the high-IQ ADHD adult seeking to create conditions for their best output. His strategies for eliminating shallow work and protecting deep work windows align closely with the productivity principles in Chapter 13. Caveat: Newport's framework assumes more consistent voluntary focus capacity than ADHD provides. The strategies require adaptation — particularly around the scheduling of deep work windows to match peak activation rather than arbitrary fixed times — but the core principles are sound and useful.

Section 3: Academic and Research Resources

For adults who want to engage with the primary research literature — the studies, meta-analyses, and review papers that underlie the clinical guidance in this manual — the following resources provide access. PubMed Central (PMC) ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc The free, publicly accessible archive of biomedical and life sciences literature maintained by the National Institutes of Health. The majority of peer-reviewed ADHD research is accessible here, either in full text or in abstract form. Most useful for: Searching for specific research cited in this manual or in other resources; exploring research on specific ADHD topics in more depth; accessing the primary literature rather than secondary summaries. Recommended search terms for key topics in this manual: adult ADHD executive function, ADHD emotional dysregulation, twice exceptional ADHD, ADHD working memory, ADHD treatment adults, ADHD exercise, ADHD sleep, rejection sensitive dysphoria ADHD. Key researchers to follow: The following researchers have produced the most substantial and reliable bodies of work on adult ADHD and related topics. Their names in a PubMed search will locate their primary publications: Russell A. Barkley, PhD — The most prolific and rigorous researcher in the field. His work on ADHD as a self-regulation disorder, executive function impairment, and the lifespan course of ADHD is foundational. His YouTube lectures — freely available — are among the most comprehensive and accessible primary-source explanations of ADHD science available. Thomas E. Brown, PhD — Particularly relevant for twice-exceptional and high-IQ presentations. His model of executive function impairment in ADHD and its application to high-functioning adults is the most directly applicable to the profile described in this manual. William Dodson, MD — The clinician most associated with the interest-based nervous system framework and with Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria. His clinical writing, accessible through the ADDitude Magazine website, provides the most detailed available account of the emotional features of ADHD in adults. Stephanie Sarkis, PhD — Clinical writing on adult ADHD presentation in high-functioning individuals, ADHD in therapeutic contexts, and ADHD and relationships.

Ned Hallowell, MD — Clinical writing on ADHD from a strengths-informed, humanistic

perspective. More accessible than technically rigorous, but valuable for the relational and emotional dimensions of the profile. Johan Wiklund, PhD — Research on the relationship between ADHD characteristics and entrepreneurship, referenced in Chapter 6. The substance use and ADHD risk meta-analysis referenced in Chapter 20. Section 4: Online Tools and Apps The following digital tools have evidence of usefulness for ADHD management. No single tool works for every ADHD adult; the recommendations here are starting points for experimentation rather than universal prescriptions. Time Management and Focus Focusmate (focusmate.com) A virtual body doubling service that pairs users with accountability partners for structured fifty-minute work sessions. Among the most consistently recommended tools in the ADHD community, with strong anecdotal support and the neurological logic described in Chapter 12 behind it. Free tier available; premium tier provides more scheduling flexibility. Forest (forestapp.cc) A focus timer app that uses a gamification mechanism — growing a virtual tree during focus periods that dies if the phone is used — to support phone-away focus periods. Effective for adults who respond to mild gamification and visual feedback. Available for iOS and Android. TimeTimer (timetimer.com) A physical and digital timer designed around visual time representation — a red disc that decreases as time elapses, making remaining time visible rather than purely numeric. Designed specifically to address time blindness; widely recommended by ADHD clinicians. Available as a physical device and as an app. Task Management Todoist (todoist.com) A task management application with natural language input, cross-platform sync, and enough structure to support external task management without requiring high executive function to maintain. Widely used in the ADHD community for its combination of simplicity and functionality. Free tier available. Things 3 (culturedcode.com/things) — Apple platforms only A well-designed task management application particularly noted for its clean interface and the cognitive ease with which tasks can be captured and organized. Premium only; Apple platforms exclusively. Frequently recommended for ADHD adults who find more feature-heavy task managers over-engineered.

A physical notebook — The analogue capture system remains one of the most reliable options for ADHD adults, particularly for immediate capture. The low friction of opening a physical notebook and writing is often lower than opening an app, authenticating, and navigating to the right location. A simple pocket notebook used consistently outperforms sophisticated apps used inconsistently. Website Blocking and Digital Focus Freedom (freedom.to) A cross-platform app and website blocker that can be scheduled in advance, locking access to specified sites and apps during designated focus periods. Particularly useful because scheduled sessions can be configured to start automatically, removing the in-the-moment decision to enable blocking. Premium subscription required. Cold Turkey (getcoldturkey.com) A website and application blocker with a reputation for being genuinely difficult to circumvent — relevant for adults whose impulse control makes easily-bypassed blocks ineffective. Free version available; premium tier provides more scheduling options. Habit Tracking Streaks (streaksapp.com) — Apple platforms only A habit tracking application designed specifically for daily habit maintenance. Its simple interface and streak visualization provide the gamification and visual feedback that support ADHD habit formation. Premium; Apple platforms exclusively. Habitica (habitica.com) A habit and task tracking application built as a role-playing game, providing points, levels, and rewards for completing habits and tasks. Effective for adults who respond strongly to gamification and find standard trackers insufficiently engaging. Free and premium tiers. Sleep and Relaxation Sleep Reset, Sleepio, or CBT-I Coach — Applications providing structured Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia protocols, which have the strongest evidence base for persistent sleep difficulties. CBT-I Coach is free and developed by the US Department of Veterans Affairs; Sleepio is a research-backed commercial product; Sleep Reset is a newer app-based program. Calm or Headspace — Guided meditation applications with evidence of usefulness for stress reduction and sleep onset. Both have free trial periods; premium subscriptions required for full

access. For ADHD adults new to mindfulness practice, guided sessions are significantly more accessible than unstructured practice. Section 5: Finding Professional Support Finding an ADHD Evaluator The most reliable routes to finding a qualified adult ADHD evaluator: The APSARD professional directory at apsard.org provides a searchable database of clinicians with specific ADHD expertise. The CHADD professional directory at chadd.org/professional-directory allows search by location and specialty. Neuropsychologists listed with the National Academy of Neuropsychology (nanonline.org) or the American Board of Professional Neuropsychology can be located through their respective directories. For complex, twice-exceptional profiles, a board-certified neuropsychologist is often the most comprehensive evaluation option. When contacting potential evaluators, asking the following questions is appropriate and informative: Do you have specific experience evaluating adult ADHD? Are you familiar with how ADHD presents in high-IQ or high-functioning adults? What does your evaluation process involve, and how long does it take? What documentation do you provide following the evaluation? Finding an ADHD Therapist Beyond general therapist directories, several resources specifically identify therapists with ADHD expertise: The CHADD professional directory includes therapists alongside evaluators and coaches. Psychology Today's therapist directory (psychologytoday.com/us/therapists) allows filtering by specialty, including ADHD. The designation does not guarantee expertise — follow-up questions about specific training and experience are appropriate. When evaluating a potential therapist, asking about specific training in ADHD, familiarity with adult presentations, and their approach to the executive function and emotional regulation components of ADHD — not just the attention symptoms — provides useful information about their preparation for this specific population.

Finding an ADHD Coach

The ACO directory at adhdcoaches.org is the primary resource for finding coaches with specific training credentials. The International Coaching Federation (coachingfederation.org) also maintains a directory that includes ADHD coaches. ADHD coaching is an unregulated field — credentials and training vary significantly. The ACO provides the most consistent standard for coach training. When evaluating a coach, asking about their specific training, their experience with adult ADHD, and whether they have experience with high-achieving or twice-exceptional adults is appropriate. Virtual coaching — conducted by video or phone — is widely available and removes the geographic limitation of in-person services. Finding Peer Support Peer support — connection with other adults who share the ADHD experience — is a meaningful complement to professional support, particularly for reducing isolation and normalizing the experience of the profile. ADDA Virtual Peer Support Groups (add.org) — Free, facilitated online support groups specifically for adults with ADHD. The virtual format makes them accessible regardless of location. CHADD local chapters (chadd.org/chadd-chapters) — In-person support groups in many cities, facilitated by trained volunteers. Online communities — Reddit communities including r/ADHD and r/ADHD_Programmers (for technology-oriented adults) are large, active communities with a substantial proportion of adult members. Quality of information varies; peer support and shared experience are the primary value rather than clinical guidance. Section 6: Citations and Evidence Notes The following notes provide additional context for the major research claims made throughout this manual.

ADHD Prevalence in Adults

The figure of approximately 2.5-4% adult prevalence cited throughout this manual is consistent across multiple large-scale epidemiological studies. The World Health Organization's World Mental Health Survey (Kessler et al., 2006, American Journal of Psychiatry) found adult ADHD prevalence of 3.4% across ten countries. A 2012 meta-analysis by Simon et al. (British Journal of Psychiatry) found global adult prevalence of 2.5%. ADHD Heritability Heritability estimates of 70-80% for ADHD are consistent across multiple twin and family studies. A 2010 meta-analysis by Faraone and Mick (Child and Adolescent Psychiatric Clinics of North America) reviewed the genetic epidemiology literature and concluded that ADHD is among the most heritable of psychiatric conditions, with heritability estimates consistently in this range. Gender Differences in Diagnosis Research on gender disparities in ADHD diagnosis is consistent and substantial. A 2020 review by Hinshaw and Scheffer in the Annual Review of Clinical Psychology documented significant differences in diagnosis rates, symptom presentation, and clinical recognition between males and females with ADHD. Quinn and Madhoo (2014) in Postgraduate Medicine reviewed the evidence on ADHD in women specifically, documenting the predominance of inattentive symptoms, greater masking, and later diagnosis age. ADHD and Substance Use The elevated rates of substance use disorders in ADHD populations are among the most consistently replicated findings in the epidemiology of ADHD. Biederman et al. (1995) in Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease documented elevated risk for substance use disorders in ADHD adults. The meta-analysis by Groenman et al. (2019) cited in Chapter 20 provides the most current comprehensive estimate of relative risk. ADHD Persistence into Adulthood Longitudinal studies following ADHD-diagnosed children into adulthood consistently find persistence rates of 50-65% at full threshold, with higher rates when subthreshold symptoms are included. Barkley et al.'s Milwaukee Longitudinal Study, published across multiple papers from the 1990s through 2000s, is among the most rigorous longitudinal investigations. A review

by Faraone et al. (2006) in American Journal of Psychiatry synthesized the longitudinal evidence, finding that ADHD persists into adulthood in the majority of cases. Exercise and ADHD The evidence for exercise's neurological effects on ADHD-relevant functioning is substantial. In addition to the Berwid and Halperin (2012) review cited above, Pontifex et al. (2013) in Journal of Abnormal Psychology demonstrated acute exercise effects on executive function specifically in ADHD populations. John Ratey's synthesis in Spark (2008) remains the most accessible account of the neurological mechanisms. CBT for Adult ADHD Multiple randomized controlled trials support the efficacy of CBT adapted for adult ADHD. Safren et al. (2010) in Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology demonstrated significant improvements over medication alone in adults receiving the CBT protocol they developed. Solanto et al. (2010) in the same journal demonstrated efficacy for their Metacognitive Therapy protocol. The meta-analysis by Knouse et al. (2017) in Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology synthesized the CBT literature, finding robust effect sizes across multiple outcome domains. A Note on Changing Information Research on ADHD is advancing rapidly. Diagnostic frameworks, treatment options, and the evidence base for specific interventions continue to evolve. The information in this manual and in these resources was accurate at the time of preparation; professional consultation is always indicated for treatment decisions, and verification of current clinical guidance through the organizations listed above is recommended for time-sensitive clinical questions. The fundamental architecture of the condition described in this manual — its neurological basis, its executive function impairments, its emotional features, and its specific interaction with high intellectual ability — is well-established and unlikely to change substantially with new research. The specific treatment options and their evidence bases are the areas most likely to have evolved since any given resource was produced. When in doubt about the currency of specific clinical guidance: check CHADD's National Resource Center, NIMH's ADHD pages, and the current literature through PubMed. These are the most reliably current sources of evidence-based clinical information.
