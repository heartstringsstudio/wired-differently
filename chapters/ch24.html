Scripts

Words for the Conversations That Matter

Why Scripts Belong in This Manual

Scripts have already appeared in Chapter 15, in the context of relationships. They are collected and expanded here because the principle behind them deserves its own dedicated treatment. The ADHD brain under stress — under social pressure, under emotional activation, in the middle of a conversation that matters — does not reliably generate its best language. The working memory that holds the full context of what needs to be said while also tracking the other person's response while also managing the emotional activation of the moment is the same working memory that ADHD impairs. The result is often communication that is less than what was intended: defensive when honesty was wanted, apologetic when clarity was needed, evasive when directness was the right call, or simply absent — the conversation avoided because the words couldn't be reliably found in the moment. Scripts address this by doing the language work in advance, during a calm period when the full cognitive resources are available, and storing the result as a retrievable tool for the moment when it is needed but hardest to produce. This is the same logic as the implementation intention, the pause protocol, and the initiation ritual — external preparation compensating for the specific impairments that ADHD produces in the moment of need. Scripts are not performances. They are not scripts in the theatrical sense, where the specific wording must be followed exactly. They are prepared language — a starting point, a floor, a reliable enough approximation of the right thing to say that the conversation can begin even when the spontaneous generation of those words is unavailable. Use them as written or adapt them freely. The words matter less than having words. Section 1: Talking to a Doctor or Clinician The clinical encounter — whether an initial evaluation, a medication review, or an ongoing treatment relationship — requires the ADHD adult to describe their experience accurately, advocate for their needs clearly, and navigate a power dynamic that can activate the very masking and compensation behaviors that make accurate description difficult. The irony is consistent: in the clinical interview, where honest self-presentation matters most, the ADHD adult is most likely to present well — verbally fluent, apparently organized, managing

the social performance of competence with all available skill — and thereby produce the least accurate picture of their daily functioning. Preparing language in advance addresses this directly. Having written specific examples of impairment before the appointment, and being willing to read from notes if verbal recall is unavailable in the moment, is legitimate and useful. Script 1A: Requesting an ADHD Evaluation For use with a primary care physician, psychiatrist, or psychologist when seeking an initial evaluation. "I'm here because I've been doing a lot of reading and research about adult ADHD, and I believe it may explain a pattern I've been living with for most of my life. I want to be evaluated properly. I'm not here with a self-diagnosis — I genuinely want a thorough assessment. But I want to describe what I'm experiencing so you have an accurate picture, and I want to be evaluated by someone with experience in adult ADHD, and specifically in how it presents in high-functioning adults where the profile can be less obvious. The main things I'm dealing with are: [insert two or three most impairing specific examples — not categories but actual instances]. These aren't new — I can trace versions of these patterns back to childhood. What would a comprehensive evaluation involve, and is that something you conduct, or would you refer me to someone with more specific expertise in adult ADHD?" Script 1B: Describing Impairment Accurately When You Present Well For use when the clinician seems to be concluding from your apparent functioning that the impairment is not significant. "I want to make sure I'm giving you an accurate picture, because I know I present well in structured settings like this one. What you're seeing right now is the product of significant compensatory effort that I've been developing for most of my life.

What's less visible is the cost behind the presentation — the all-nighters behind the deadlines I've met, the elaborate systems I build and abandon, the amount of energy that goes into appearing organized when I'm not. Specifically, in the last [time period], here is what my actual daily functioning has looked like: [specific examples — not impressions but concrete instances, preferably written down in advance]. I'm telling you this because I've been in evaluations before where I presented as functional and left without an accurate picture being formed. I want to make sure that doesn't happen here." Script 1C: Asking About Medication Options For use when discussing medication with a prescribing clinician for the first time, or when current medication is inadequate. "I'd like to talk about medication options. I've read a fair amount about stimulant and non-stimulant treatments for adult ADHD, and I understand there's significant individual variation in response. Can you walk me through what you would typically consider given my specific profile? Specifically, I want to understand: what are the options, what are the differences between them, and what does the titration process look like — how will we know if something is working and how will we adjust if it isn't? I also want to flag some specific things I'm dealing with that might be relevant to the choice: [sleep difficulties, anxiety, history of specific concerns, etc.] I'm not asking you to prescribe a specific medication — I'm asking to understand the decision we're making together so I can be a useful participant in it." Script 1D: Reporting That Current Treatment Isn't Working For use at a follow-up appointment when medication or other treatment is not producing adequate benefit. "I want to give you an honest update because I think we need to revisit the current approach.

What I'm noticing is: [specific description of what isn't working — not just 'it's not helping' but the specific impairments that remain]. On the positive side: [anything that has improved, even partially]. I'm not sure whether this is a dose issue, a timing issue, or whether a different medication might be a better fit — I'd like your guidance on how to think about that. What I don't want to do is stay on something that isn't adequately addressing the specific problems we're trying to address, or accept partial improvement as the best available result without exploring whether something different would work better. What options do you think are worth considering?" Script 1E: Discussing Emotional Dysregulation Specifically For use when raising the emotional regulation features of ADHD with a clinician who may be focusing primarily on attention. "I want to raise something that isn't usually the first thing discussed in ADHD appointments, but that I think is actually one of the most impairing aspects for me personally — the emotional component. I experience significant emotional intensity, a low frustration threshold, and what I understand is sometimes called rejection sensitive dysphoria — a very strong response to perceived criticism or rejection that is disproportionate to the situation and genuinely difficult to manage. This affects my relationships and my professional life in ways that the attention-focused parts of my ADHD management haven't addressed. I'd like to discuss whether there are treatment options — medication adjustments, or therapy approaches — that specifically address this, rather than treating it as a secondary issue or a separate diagnosis. Is that something you can help with, or would you refer me to someone who specializes in this?" Section 2: Asking for Accommodations Requesting accommodations — in workplaces, educational settings, and other institutional contexts — requires a specific combination of self-advocacy, clarity about functional needs, and the ability to navigate organizational processes that can feel impersonal or resistant. The scripts here are written for adult contexts. Adapt the formality level to the specific relationship and institutional culture.

Script 2A: Requesting Workplace Accommodations Formally

For use in a conversation with HR or a manager, following a formal disclosure of ADHD diagnosis. "I wanted to have a conversation about some workplace adjustments that would help me perform at my best. I have a formal diagnosis of ADHD, and I'm happy to provide documentation from my clinician. I want to be clear that I'm not asking for a reduced standard of work — I'm asking for adjustments to how and where I do the work, so that the neurological profile I'm managing doesn't create disadvantages that have nothing to do with my actual capability. The specific accommodations I'd like to discuss are: [list, drawn from Chapter 16, tailored to your specific role and impairments]. I've thought carefully about these, and I believe each one would make a material difference to my functioning without creating significant burden on the team or organization. I'm happy to discuss each one specifically, explain the functional basis for it, and work with you on implementation. Can we go through them?" Script 2B: Requesting Informal Accommodations Without Formal Disclosure For use when requesting adjustments through working-style language, without naming the diagnosis. "I wanted to ask for a couple of adjustments to how we work together that I think would meaningfully improve my output. First: I do my best cognitively demanding work in the morning, and my focus is significantly less reliable in the afternoon. If there's flexibility to schedule our collaborative and meeting time toward later in the day and protect my mornings for deep work, I think you'd see a real difference in what I produce. Second: I track and retain complex information significantly better when it comes in writing rather than verbally. If you'd be willing to follow up verbal briefings on important projects with a

brief email summary, I'll be less likely to miss things and more likely to execute correctly the first time. These aren't large asks, and I genuinely think they'd make a difference. Is that something we can try?" Script 2C: Requesting Educational Accommodations For use with a disability services office, student services coordinator, or academic advisor. "I'm here to request accommodations for a documented disability. I have ADHD — I have documentation from a qualified clinician that I'm prepared to provide. I'd like to discuss what accommodations are available, and specifically which ones would address the functional impairments that affect my academic performance most directly. The areas where I experience the most significant impairment are: processing speed and task initiation under timed conditions, attention sustainability in low-stimulation testing environments, and organization and follow-through on extended projects. Based on what I understand is typically available, the accommodations I believe would be most relevant are: [extended time on examinations, reduced-distraction testing environment, extended deadlines for major projects, note-taking support for lectures — identify what applies to your situation]. Can you walk me through the process for formalizing these, and what documentation you need from me?" Section 3: Explaining ADHD to People Who Matter The decision about whether and how to explain ADHD to partners, family members, friends, or colleagues is individual and complex — covered in the relationship and career chapters. The scripts here are for people who have decided that explanation is appropriate and useful, and who need language that conveys the reality accurately without either minimizing it or making it sound like an excuse.

Script 3A: Explaining ADHD to a Partner Who Doesn't Know

This script is intentionally conversational rather than clinical. The goal is authentic disclosure, not a medical presentation. "I want to tell you something about myself that I think will help explain some things you've probably noticed — and some things that have probably frustrated you. I have ADHD. I know that sounds like it's just about being distracted or hyper, but the actual experience is quite different from that, and more pervasive. What it actually means for me is: I genuinely struggle to initiate tasks even when I want to do them and know they're important. My working memory is unreliable — which is why I forget things that matter to you, not because they don't matter but because my internal reminder system doesn't work. My relationship with time is genuinely different — deadlines that are abstract feel unreal until they're immediate, which is why I operate in crisis mode more often than I should. And my emotions arrive faster and at higher intensity than I'd choose, which I know has been difficult at times. I've been managing all of this, largely on my own, for a long time — without really understanding what it was or having the right support for it. I want to be honest with you about it because I think it explains a lot, and because managing it well is easier with someone who understands what's actually happening rather than what it looks like from outside. I'm not telling you this to make excuses for the things that have been hard. I'm telling you because I want us to figure out how to handle those things together, now that there's an accurate name for what they are. Do you want to ask me anything, or just sit with it for a bit?" Script 3B: Explaining ADHD to a Parent or Family Member Often the most emotionally complex disclosure, because it may involve reframing a shared history. "I want to talk to you about something I've learned about myself that I think reframes some of my history — and maybe some of yours with me. I've recently [been diagnosed with / come to understand that I have] ADHD. Not the childhood stereotype — the adult version, the one that was never caught because I was smart enough to mostly get through things, even when getting through them cost me more than it should have.

I'm not telling you this to relitigate the past or assign blame — I know you were working with the information you had, and so was I. What I want is for you to have a more accurate picture of what was happening, and what is still happening, so that the story we've both been telling makes more sense. The things that looked like laziness, or lack of effort, or not living up to potential — those weren't what they appeared to be. They were a specific neurological profile that wasn't identified, in a person who was working harder than anyone could see to manage something they didn't have the words for. I'm telling you because you matter to me, and because carrying the version of the story where those things were my fault is something I'm working on putting down. I'd like you to know the more accurate version." Script 3C: Explaining ADHD to a Close Friend Less formal, more relational — suited for close friendships where honesty and shared understanding are the goal. "Can I tell you something about myself that I haven't talked about much? I've been figuring out — [or: I was recently diagnosed with] — ADHD, and it's explained a lot about me that I think you've probably observed without having a name for. The forgetting things, the last-minute everything, the way I can be completely absorbed in something for hours and then unable to do something basic. The emotional intensity sometimes. The trail of unfinished projects. The way I'm fully present with you in one conversation and seem to be somewhere else in the next. It's not that any of that was intentional or that it reflects how much I care. It's a specific way my brain works that I'm still learning to manage properly. I wanted you to know partly because you're someone I trust with real things, and partly because it might make some of my patterns make more sense — and maybe make them less annoying to be around when I know you understand what's actually going on. You don't need to do anything differently. I mostly just wanted you to know." Section 4: Asking for Help Asking for help is, for many high-IQ ADHD adults, among the hardest things in this manual to do. The internalized belief that they should be able to manage independently — the shame

about needing accommodation for something their intelligence should handle — makes the request feel like an admission of fundamental inadequacy rather than a reasonable response to a real limitation. The scripts here treat asking for help as the practical, non-shameful action that it is. Script 4A: Asking a Colleague for Accountability Support "I want to ask you something that might seem slightly unusual, but I think it would genuinely help me and I think you're someone I can ask. I work better when I have a specific person expecting something from me at a specific time — it's just how my brain is wired. I wondered whether you'd be willing to be that person for [specific project or task area]. What I'm asking for is simple: I'd tell you what I'm going to have done by when, and you'd check in with me at that point. Not supervise or manage — just ask. The accountability of knowing someone is going to ask makes a real functional difference for me. It doesn't have to be reciprocal, though I'm happy for it to be if there's something I can offer in return. Is that something you'd be willing to try?" Script 4B: Asking a Partner for Specific Structural Support "I want to ask for something specific, and I want to frame it as a genuine request rather than a complaint about how things are currently working. One of the things that helps me most is external reminders for things that are important but not urgent — the kind of thing my working memory drops when it isn't right in front of me. I know it would be more ideal if my internal reminder system worked reliably. It doesn't, and I'm building systems to compensate, but having you as a backup for [specific category — appointments, commitments, important dates] would genuinely help. I'm not asking you to manage me — I'm asking for a specific, bounded thing that would reduce one of the recurring problems between us. Would you be willing to try it for a month and see if it helps?"

Script 4C: Asking a Manager for Help Structuring Work

"I want to have a conversation about how I work best, because I think there are some adjustments that would make me significantly more effective and I'd rather discuss them directly than let the pattern continue. I'm at my best when I have clear, specific deadlines and intermediate check-in points on longer projects — not because I need supervision, but because concrete external structure is what activates my best work. When timelines are long and vague, I tend to do my least consistent work. When there are specific milestones with real dates, I tend to do my best. Would you be willing to build our project conversations around that structure? Specifically: clear deliverables, specific dates, and a brief check-in at each milestone point. I think it would change what you see from me on longer-timeline work. I'm raising this proactively because I know it's not the default way projects are managed here, and I didn't want it to become a problem before I asked for what would prevent it." Section 5: Setting Boundaries Boundaries are difficult for many ADHD adults — the impulsivity that makes saying yes automatic, the RSD that makes saying no feel like risking rejection, and the poor time estimation that leads to overcommitment. These scripts address the specific situations where boundary-setting is most difficult. Script 5A: Declining a New Commitment "I appreciate you thinking of me for this, and I want to give you an honest answer rather than a reflexive yes that I can't follow through on. Right now, my capacity is genuinely committed. Taking on something additional would mean doing it less well than it deserves, or letting down something I'm already committed to. Neither of those serves you or the work. I'm saying no now so that I don't have to do it badly later. I hope you'll come back to me when my capacity is different."

Script 5B: Renegotiating an Overcommitted Schedule

"I need to have an honest conversation about what I've committed to, because I've overcommitted and I'd rather address it directly than let things fall apart on the back end. Here is what I currently have on my plate: [list]. Here is what I can realistically deliver well: [honest subset]. I'd like to talk with you about which of the remaining items can be moved, delayed, handed off, or reduced in scope — rather than have all of them suffer. I know this isn't the conversation you were expecting. I think it's better to have it now than after the deadlines have passed." Script 5C: Setting a Boundary Around Interruptions "I want to ask for something that would make a real difference to my work quality. When I'm in a focused work period, interruptions are genuinely costly for me — not just the time the interruption takes, but the recovery time afterward. I lose the thread in a way that takes significantly longer to rebuild than the interruption itself. Would you be willing to use [specific channel — a specific messaging tag, an email instead of a knock, a specific signal that indicates I'm in focus mode] when the matter isn't urgent? For urgent things, interrupt freely. For things that can wait twenty minutes, the alternative channel would protect a real resource for me. I'd be genuinely grateful for this." Section 6: Repairing After Failures These scripts are for after the fact — after a commitment was missed, a response was sharp, an important thing was forgotten, a promise was not kept. They are designed for repair that is genuine rather than formulaic: specific, responsible, and action-oriented rather than purely apologetic.

Script 6A: After Forgetting Something Important

"I need to talk to you about [specific thing forgotten or missed], and I don't want to just apologize and move on, because I know that's happened before and the pattern matters as much as this instance. I forgot, and I understand that this probably didn't feel like a minor thing. The honest reason is that my working memory doesn't retain things that aren't externalized, and this wasn't externalized. That's a systems problem on my end, not a reflection of how important it is to me. What I'm going to do differently is [specific structural change: calendar reminder, asking you to follow up, writing it in a specific place I check]. Not 'I'll try harder to remember' — that approach hasn't worked and I'm not going to promise it again. A specific, structural change. I'm sorry for the impact this had. Is there anything I can do now to address it?" Script 6B: After an Emotional Overreaction "I want to come back to what happened earlier, because I don't think my reaction was okay and I want to acknowledge that properly. What I did was [specific behavior — not a category, but the actual thing]. I was in a heightened state and I directed that at you in a way that wasn't fair, regardless of what else was happening. You didn't deserve that response. I want to be clear that my reaction had nothing to do with [what it might have seemed to be about]. What it was actually about was [honest description of internal state, if useful and if it helps rather than excuses]. I'm working on [specific thing — emotional regulation strategies, therapy, a specific pattern]. I know that doesn't undo what happened. I'm telling you because I want you to know it's something I'm taking seriously, not something I'm just apologizing for reflexively. I'm sorry." Script 6C: After a Pattern of Unreliability This script is for the larger conversation — not a single incident but an acknowledgment of a recurring pattern.

"I want to have a harder conversation than a single apology, because I think we're past the point where a single apology is adequate. The pattern over [time period] has been [honest description: late, unreliable, promising and not delivering, inconsistently present]. I know that. I've watched it happen and I haven't been able to change it as much as I've intended to. I want to say two things. First: I understand the impact this has had. Not just the individual incidents but the accumulation of them — what it's like to not be able to count on me in the specific ways you've needed to. That's a real cost and you've been carrying it. Second: I'm not asking you to trust that it will be different based on my saying so. I'm asking whether you'd be willing to see whether it becomes different based on what I actually do, over time, with specific structural changes rather than renewed intentions. [Describe specific changes: accountability system, professional support, structural accommodations.] I understand if the answer is that you've reached the limit of what you can extend toward this. That's a legitimate position. I wanted to have the honest conversation first." Script 6D: After Missing a Professional Deadline "I need to come to you directly about [deadline] because I'd rather address it with honesty than with a series of updates that don't get to the real situation. I missed the deadline. Here is where the work currently stands: [honest status]. Here is my realistic completion date given current status: [specific date, conservatively estimated]. I'm not going to list the reasons in a way that sounds like a justification. What I want to do is give you accurate information about the timeline, ask what I need to understand about the downstream impact of the delay, and tell you what specifically I'm doing to make sure the remaining work is completed by the date I've given you. What do you need from me right now?" Section 7: Navigating Specific High-Stakes Situations

Script 7A: Requesting a Meeting to Discuss Performance Concerns Proactively

For use when you know performance has been inconsistent and you want to address it before the formal review. "I'd like to schedule time with you specifically to talk about my performance and what I need to be doing differently. I'm raising this proactively because I want to have this conversation from a position of honesty and forward-focus rather than in response to a formal concern. I know that [specific area] has not been where it should be. I want to understand clearly what the impact has been, what the expectations are going forward, and what support is available that I might not be using. I'd rather have a direct conversation now than let the situation continue to deteriorate and address it only when it becomes a formal process. Can we find time this week?" Script 7B: Disclosing ADHD Mid-Conversation When It Becomes Relevant For situations where ADHD becomes relevant in a conversation that didn't begin as a disclosure conversation. "Can I be honest with you about something that's relevant here? I have ADHD — which I know doesn't always look like the stereotype — and what you're describing [or what just happened] is related to how it affects my functioning. I'm not raising this as an excuse for [specific thing]. I'm raising it because I think it gives you more accurate information about what's actually happening and what would actually help, versus what the situation might look like from outside. What I'd find most useful right now is [specific thing]. Does that work for you?" Script 7C: Declining to Be Undermined by the Lazy Narrative For use when someone implies or states directly that the difficulties are about attitude or effort. "I want to push back on that framing, because I think it's inaccurate and because accepting an inaccurate story about myself doesn't serve either of us.

The implication that this is about effort or motivation — that if I cared enough or tried hard enough the pattern would change — doesn't match what I know about my own history. I have been trying, consistently, for a long time. The pattern has persisted not because of insufficient effort but because effort alone isn't the solution to the specific thing I'm working with. I'm not looking for sympathy and I'm not making an excuse. I'm asking for the more accurate framework, because the accurate framework points toward what would actually help rather than toward more of what hasn't been working. What would be more useful to discuss is [specific, constructive alternative direction]." A Note on Using These Scripts Read through these scripts during a calm period — not in anticipation of an immediately needed conversation. Mark the ones most relevant to your current situation. Adapt the language to your voice; the scripts as written are a starting point, not a required text. When a relevant situation arises, return to the script before the conversation rather than during it. If you can, write a brief personalized version — one or two key sentences that capture the most important thing you need to say — and have it accessible if verbal memory fails under the pressure of the moment. Saying something imperfect is almost always better than the silence that avoidance produces. The script is permission to say the imperfect version — to begin the conversation that needs to happen rather than waiting for the moment when you feel ready, which the ADHD brain's avoidance of emotionally complex tasks means may never arrive on its own. The conversations in this chapter are worth having. The relationships, the professional situations, and the self-advocacy they address are real and consequential. Preparing the language in advance is not weakness. It is the same strategy that makes every other element of effective ADHD management possible: building external structure where internal reliability falls short, so that the life you're trying to live has the tools it actually needs.
