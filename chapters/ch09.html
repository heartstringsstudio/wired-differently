Chapter 9

Diagnosis and Professional Evaluation

What a Good Evaluation Actually Looks Like — And How to Get One

The previous chapter explained why diagnosis is so often delayed. This one is about what to do about that — specifically, what a thorough, accurate evaluation of adult ADHD looks like, who is qualified to conduct it, what the process involves, and how to navigate it as a high-IQ adult whose profile doesn't match the standard prototype. This chapter is practical by design. It is not a substitute for professional evaluation — that point cannot be overstated. But it is a guide to understanding what good evaluation looks like, so that you can seek it deliberately, advocate for yourself effectively within it, and recognize when an evaluation has been insufficient. What Diagnosis Is — and Isn't ADHD diagnosis is a clinical judgment, not a test result. There is no blood test, brain scan, or single psychological instrument that diagnoses ADHD. Neuroimaging studies have documented consistent brain differences in ADHD populations, but these findings are at the group level and are not currently useful for individual diagnosis. Genetic testing is not part of clinical evaluation. Computerized attention tests can provide supporting data but are not diagnostic on their own. What produces a diagnosis is a comprehensive clinical picture — assembled from multiple sources of information, evaluated against established diagnostic criteria, and interpreted by a qualified clinician with expertise in ADHD and its presentations. This has important implications. It means that diagnosis requires a skilled evaluator, adequate time, and a sufficiently comprehensive process. A brief screening by a general practitioner, a self-report questionnaire completed online, or a single appointment focused primarily on the presenting complaint is unlikely to produce a fully accurate picture — particularly for a high-IQ adult whose profile is complex and whose compensation strategies obscure the surface presentation. It also means that a negative evaluation — being told you don't have ADHD — is not necessarily definitive. A negative finding from an inadequate evaluation using inappropriate instruments by a clinician without specific adult ADHD expertise is meaningfully less reliable than a negative finding from a comprehensive evaluation conducted by a specialist. If the former has happened and the profile described in this manual resonates strongly, seeking a second opinion from a more appropriate provider is a reasonable step.

The Diagnostic Criteria The formal diagnostic criteria for ADHD in adults come from the DSM-5, the diagnostic manual published by the American Psychiatric Association and used as the primary standard in the United States and widely internationally. The ICD-11, published by the World Health Organization, uses comparable criteria and is the primary standard in many other countries. The DSM-5 criteria for adult ADHD require: Symptom threshold For adults, five or more symptoms from either the inattentive symptom list or the hyperactive-impulsive symptom list — or both — must be present. This is a lower threshold than for children, reflecting the recognition that symptom expression often changes with age. The inattentive symptoms include: difficulty sustaining attention in tasks or activities; frequently failing to give close attention to details; not following through on instructions or failing to finish tasks; difficulty organizing tasks and activities; avoiding or being reluctant to engage in tasks requiring sustained mental effort; frequently losing things necessary for tasks; being easily distracted by extraneous stimuli; frequently forgetting daily activities. The hyperactive-impulsive symptoms include: fidgeting or tapping; leaving seat in situations where remaining seated is expected; running or climbing in inappropriate situations (in adults, often reported as restlessness); being unable to engage quietly in leisure activities; being "on the go" or acting as if "driven by a motor"; talking excessively; blurting out answers before a question is completed; difficulty waiting one's turn; interrupting or intruding on others. Developmental onset Several symptoms must have been present before age 12. This criterion exists because ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition — it is present from early development, not acquired in adulthood. For late-diagnosed adults, establishing this requires retrospective history: memories of childhood, school records if available, and ideally collateral information from parents or others who knew the person as a child. This criterion is where high-IQ adults often run into difficulty. Their childhood symptoms may not have been recognized as ADHD symptoms at the time — they were the dreamy child, the underachiever despite obvious ability, the one who couldn't get organized but somehow got through. Reconstruction of the childhood picture requires a clinician who knows what to look for in this profile.

Pervasiveness Symptoms must be present in two or more settings — not only at work, not only at home, but across multiple contexts. This criterion distinguishes ADHD from situational difficulties that might arise from a specific stressor or environment. Impairment Symptoms must clearly interfere with or reduce the quality of social, academic, or occupational functioning. As discussed in Chapter 8, this criterion is problematic for high-IQ adults who appear to function adequately by external standards while being significantly impaired relative to their own capacity. A skilled evaluator will explore impairment in depth — including the effort required to produce outcomes, not just the outcomes themselves. Not better explained by another condition Symptoms must not be better explained by another mental health condition — schizophrenia, another psychotic disorder, mood disorder, anxiety disorder, dissociative disorder, personality disorder, substance intoxication, or withdrawal. This requires differential diagnosis: a process of systematically ruling out other explanations for the symptom picture. Importantly, the presence of anxiety, depression, or other comorbidities does not rule out ADHD — it requires careful clinical judgment about which symptoms are attributable to which condition, and whether multiple diagnoses are warranted. What a Comprehensive Evaluation Includes A thorough evaluation for adult ADHD — particularly in a high-IQ individual with a complex profile — typically involves several components. Not every evaluation will include all of these, and their relative weight varies by setting and clinician. But understanding what a comprehensive evaluation looks like allows you to identify when an evaluation has been insufficient. Clinical interview The clinical interview is the centerpiece of ADHD evaluation. A skilled interviewer will explore:

  • Current symptoms across multiple domains: attention, impulse control, organization, working memory, emotional regulation, time management
  • Current impairment in work or educational settings, relationships, and daily life management
  • Developmental history: what childhood was like, school performance and experience, early behavioral patterns, whether symptoms were present before age 12
  • Family history of ADHD or related conditions, given the strong heritability of the condition
  • Medical history: any conditions that might explain or contribute to the symptom picture
  • Psychiatric history: past and current mental health conditions, prior diagnoses and treatments
  • Substance use history: relevant both as a potential comorbidity and as something to rule out as an alternative explanation for symptoms
  • Sleep patterns: given the strong relationship between ADHD and sleep dysfunction
  • Life context: current stressors, life circumstances, and environmental factors

A comprehensive clinical interview for adult ADHD typically takes at least sixty to ninety minutes. A fifteen-minute appointment is not an adequate evaluation, regardless of who conducts it. Standardized rating scales Rating scales are structured questionnaires that assess symptom frequency and severity. Commonly used instruments in adult ADHD evaluation include:

  • The Conners Adult ADHD Rating Scales (CAARS): a widely used, well-validated tool for assessing adult ADHD symptoms across multiple domains
  • The Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (ASRS): a brief screener developed in collaboration with the World Health Organization, useful as an initial screening tool but not as a standalone diagnostic instrument
  • The Brown ADD Rating Scales: assess executive function difficulties across multiple domains and are useful for capturing the full functional picture
  • The Weiss Functional Impairment Rating Scale (WFIRS): assesses impairment across multiple life domains, which is particularly useful for documenting the real-world impact of symptoms

Many evaluators also use observer-report versions of rating scales — completed by a partner, family member, or colleague who knows the person well — which provide an external perspective on symptom presentation and help address the compensation and masking effects that self-report alone may miss. For high-IQ adults, it is worth being aware that self-report scales may underestimate impairment due to compensation effects — as discussed in Chapter 8. A clinician who is aware of this

dynamic will weigh the scales accordingly and will not rely on them as the primary basis for a diagnostic decision. Neuropsychological testing Not all ADHD evaluations include neuropsychological testing, but for high-IQ adults with complex presentations, it provides information that clinical interview and rating scales alone cannot. Neuropsychological testing assesses cognitive functioning across multiple domains using standardized, performance-based measures. In the context of ADHD evaluation, relevant domains include: Cognitive ability testing: Assessment of overall intellectual functioning, typically using instruments such as the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS-IV or WAIS-V). This establishes the ability baseline that is essential for interpreting other findings — a critical point for high-IQ individuals, where absolute scores on executive function measures may be adequate in comparative terms while representing significant impairment relative to the person's own general ability. Executive function assessment: Performance-based measures of working memory, cognitive flexibility, inhibitory control, processing speed, and planning. These include tasks such as digit span (working memory), Trail Making Test (cognitive flexibility and processing speed), Stroop Color-Word Test (inhibitory control), and various planning and problem-solving tasks. Attention assessment: Continuous performance tests — tasks requiring sustained attention over time — can reveal attention lapses and variability that symptom report alone may not capture. Academic achievement testing: Relevant when learning differences are suspected alongside ADHD, or when the profile suggests possible dyslexia, dyscalculia, or other specific learning differences. The specific pattern of neuropsychological findings in high-IQ ADHD adults often shows a characteristic profile: high scores on measures of general intelligence and verbal ability, alongside relative weaknesses in working memory, processing speed, and certain executive function domains. This discrepancy — known as intra-individual variability — is clinically meaningful and may not be apparent without systematic testing. It is important to note that neuropsychological testing can produce false negatives in high-IQ ADHD adults. Testing is conducted in a structured, one-on-one environment with a novel examiner and high-interest task demands — conditions that partially activate the ADHD motivational system. The person may perform significantly better in the testing environment than in naturalistic settings. A skilled neuropsychologist will account for this in their interpretation,

looking not just at absolute scores but at performance variability, error patterns, and the relationship between ability and executive function measures. Collateral information Where available and appropriate, information from people who knew the person in childhood — parents, siblings — or who observe them currently — partners, close friends, colleagues — provides valuable external perspective that supplements self-report and addresses the limitations of single-session clinical assessment. Old school records, if accessible, can provide useful documentary evidence of childhood symptom patterns — particularly the characteristic profile of inconsistent performance, variable teacher comments, and the gap between apparent ability and actual output. Differential diagnosis and comorbidity assessment A comprehensive evaluation includes systematic consideration of conditions that might explain or contribute to the presenting symptoms. These include:

  • Anxiety disorders: Anxiety can produce concentration difficulties, restlessness, and avoidance that overlap with ADHD symptoms. The distinction requires careful exploration of the nature and context of the symptoms.
  • Depression and bipolar spectrum disorders: Depression produces concentration impairment, psychomotor changes, and executive dysfunction. Bipolar spectrum conditions can produce periods of high energy, reduced need for sleep, and impulsive behavior that overlap with ADHD hyperactivity. Both require careful differentiation.
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder: ASD and ADHD overlap substantially in symptom presentation and can be comorbid. Features including social communication differences, sensory sensitivities, and rigid thinking patterns may warrant specific assessment.
  • Trauma and PTSD: Trauma responses can produce concentration difficulties, emotional dysregulation, hypervigilance, and avoidance that overlap significantly with ADHD symptoms. This requires careful exploration of trauma history and the context and phenomenology of symptoms.
  • Sleep disorders: Obstructive sleep apnea, restless leg syndrome, and circadian rhythm disorders can all produce concentration, attention, and executive function difficulties. A sleep history should be part of any comprehensive ADHD evaluation.
  • Thyroid dysfunction and other medical conditions: Several medical conditions produce cognitive symptoms that overlap with ADHD. Relevant medical history and, where indicated, laboratory evaluation should be considered.
  • Substance use: Both active substance use and withdrawal can produce ADHD-like symptoms. History of substance use and its relationship to symptom patterns is relevant to assessment.

The goal of differential diagnosis is not to find a reason to not diagnose ADHD. It is to ensure that the full clinical picture is accurate — that what is ADHD is identified as ADHD, that what is something else is identified correctly, and that comorbid conditions are all identified and addressed rather than one being treated while others are missed. Who Is Qualified to Evaluate In the United States, ADHD in adults can be evaluated and diagnosed by:

  • Psychiatrists: Medical doctors (MDs or DOs) specializing in mental health. Can prescribe medication and conduct evaluations. Training and experience in adult ADHD varies significantly.
  • Psychologists: Doctoral-level clinicians (PhDs or PsyDs) trained in assessment and psychotherapy. Cannot prescribe medication in most U.S. states. Clinical and neuropsychologists are often best positioned to conduct comprehensive evaluations including neuropsychological testing.
  • Neuropsychologists: Psychologists with specialized training in brain-behavior relationships and neuropsychological assessment. Often the most comprehensive option for complex profiles including twice-exceptional adults.
  • Licensed clinical social workers, licensed professional counselors, and licensed marriage and family therapists: Can conduct evaluations in some states and contexts, though scope of practice varies. Typically not the primary evaluator for complex profiles.
  • Primary care physicians and nurse practitioners: Can diagnose and treat ADHD, and in many areas are the most accessible entry point. For straightforward presentations, this may be adequate. For complex, high-IQ, and potentially twice-exceptional profiles, a specialist evaluation is typically more appropriate.

The critical variable is not just licensure but specific expertise. An evaluator with extensive experience in adult ADHD, familiarity with twice-exceptional presentations, and training in the specific ways that high intellectual ability interacts with ADHD symptom presentation is significantly better positioned to conduct an accurate evaluation than a generalist clinician regardless of their overall qualifications. When seeking evaluation, it is entirely appropriate to ask about the evaluator's specific experience with adult ADHD, their familiarity with high-IQ or twice-exceptional presentations, and the typical components of their evaluation process. A clinician who is well-suited to this evaluation will welcome these questions. One who is not may reveal that in their response. Preparing for an Evaluation Preparation matters. Not as a performance — not to make yourself look more impaired than you are or to influence the outcome in a particular direction — but because a well-prepared

evaluation produces better information, and better information produces more accurate conclusions. Useful preparation includes: Gathering developmental history. Think carefully about childhood: what school was like, how you were described by teachers, what was easy and what was consistently difficult, whether you were identified as gifted or struggling or both, patterns of inconsistency that recurred across years. If parents or siblings are accessible and willing, asking them for their memories of your childhood can be useful. Old school records, if they exist and are accessible, can provide documentary evidence. Documenting current impairment specifically. Before the evaluation, spend time writing down specific, concrete examples of how the symptoms affect your current life — not general descriptions but actual incidents. The email that has been sitting in the drafts folder for six days. The appointment that was missed. The project that has been 80% complete for three months. The conversation you can describe the feeling of being present in but cannot recall the content of. Specific examples are more clinically useful than general characterizations. Noting when symptoms are better and worse. The context-dependence of ADHD impairment is clinically meaningful. Documenting when you function better — under urgency, in novel environments, with external structure, in one-on-one conversations — and when you function worse helps the evaluator understand the full pattern. Bringing someone who knows you well, if appropriate. If a partner, close friend, or family member observes your daily functioning and would be willing to participate, their perspective — even briefly — provides external validation of the picture you describe and partially addresses the masking problem. Being honest about compensation. The most useful thing you can do in a clinical evaluation is describe not just the outcomes of your functioning but what it takes to produce those outcomes. The all-nighters behind the deadline met. The preparation required for the meeting that looked fluid. The systems that worked briefly and then stopped. The effort behind the appearance of competence. This is the information most likely to be missing from the evaluator's picture, and it is the most important information for accurately characterizing the profile. Self-Report Versus Professional Diagnosis Before this chapter closes, one distinction deserves direct, clear address. Self-recognition — reading this manual, identifying with the profile described, recognizing your experience in the patterns outlined — is meaningful. It is not the same as a professional diagnosis, and it is not a substitute for one.

A professional diagnosis is not primarily a label. It is a clinical judgment, made by a qualified professional with access to comprehensive information about your history and functioning, that has direct implications for treatment access, workplace accommodation, educational accommodation, and insurance coverage. These are practical realities with real consequences. Self-recognition has its own value — it can reduce shame, provide a framework for self-understanding, and motivate help-seeking. But it can also be wrong. Other conditions produce similar symptom pictures. Accurate diagnosis of comorbidities matters for treatment planning. The specific nature of your profile — whether ADHD is the primary condition, one of several, or not present at all — has treatment implications that matter. The goal of this chapter, and of this manual, is not to convince you that you have ADHD. It is to provide enough accurate information that if the profile fits, you pursue proper evaluation — and that when you do, you seek an evaluation that is comprehensive enough to produce reliable results. That evaluation is worth doing. Whatever it finds. After the Evaluation A comprehensive evaluation produces one of several outcomes: An ADHD diagnosis — alone or alongside identified comorbidities — with recommendations for treatment. This is the beginning of a process, not the end of one. What to do with the diagnosis is the subject of the chapters ahead. A diagnosis of a different condition that explains the presenting symptoms. This is not a negative outcome. An accurate diagnosis of the condition actually driving the difficulties is directly useful, regardless of whether that condition is ADHD. A finding of multiple conditions — ADHD plus comorbidities requiring their own treatment attention. Common in the twice-exceptional profile. The treatment plan then needs to address the full picture. An unclear or inconclusive picture requiring additional assessment. This is a legitimate outcome. Complex profiles sometimes require more information — further testing, monitoring over time, or specialist consultation — before a reliable conclusion can be reached. In any of these cases, the evaluation is the beginning of a conversation between you and the clinician, not a definitive pronouncement. Ask questions. Ask what the findings mean in practice. Ask what the recommended next steps are and why. Ask what would be watched for over time and what would change the picture.

You are not a passive recipient of a verdict. You are a person seeking accurate information about your own brain, in order to make better decisions about your own life. That is exactly what this process is for.
